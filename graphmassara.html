Biography

The intricacies of the English language have always come very naturally to me. Though I have an especially keen eye for grammar, I do not tend to think of proofreading or editing as “work.” It’s something more satisfying than grueling, like straightening a bookshelf.
I worked for the Daily Californian for one life-changing year, rising in rank from a general assignment reporter to a full-time editorial position. On my inaugural night as news editor, the verdict for Mike Brown's shooter was announced, and Berkeley exploded into protest. In the nine months that followed, I covered everything from the mundane to the extraordinary, from murders and power outages to student-body elections. It was easily the most stressful period of my life, but just as easily the most invigorating; I am lucky to have found my calling at an early age.
But in time, my other priorities caught up with me, and I needed a change of pace. On a whim, I posted an ad for copy editing services on a UC Berkeley student page. The response I received absolutely floored me. I have since offered my skills to people from all walks of life – foreign exchange students still struggling with their second language, disadvantaged teens from Oakland hoping to get into prestigious universities, nonprofits, startups, news aggregators and columnists. I find it to be deeply gratifying work; in studying the idiosyncrasies of my native tongue, I have both improved my own writing abilities and gained an appreciation for the subjectivity of “proper” English. My time working for Newsela, an educational tech startup, has especially helped me solidify those same skills. I know I have a long way to go before I can call myself an expert, but I come just a bit closer with every typo I catch.
